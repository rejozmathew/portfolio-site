# The Prepayment Paradox: Using Decision Trees to Optimize Lending Portfolio Acquisition

There is a counterintuitive truth in consumer lending that most people outside the industry never encounter: **your most creditworthy borrowers can be the worst for portfolio economics**. They borrow, they make every payment on time, their credit scores improve — and then they refinance somewhere else within the first year. You spent real money to acquire them, and you barely recouped origination costs before the loan vanished.

This is the prepayment paradox, and it sits at the intersection of credit risk, marketing efficiency, and portfolio management. In this article, I walk through how we built a system to detect these high-prepayment-risk borrowers *before acquisition* and suppress them from solicitation campaigns — using segmentation trees, credit bureau features, and a closed-loop feedback architecture that paid for itself many times over.

---

## Why Prepayment Matters More Than You Think

In a home equity lending portfolio, the **Conditional Prepayment Rate (CPR)** — the annualized rate at which borrowers pay off their loans ahead of schedule — is one of the most important metrics for forecasting revenue and managing securitization assumptions. CPR is a function of two components: unscheduled accelerated principal payments and full loan payoffs (pay-in-full or PIF events). In most portfolios, PIFs account for the vast majority of prepayment activity.

The economic impact is straightforward but significant. Every early payoff means lost interest income over the remaining loan term. When you factor in origination costs — underwriting, title work, appraisals, solicitation campaigns — a loan that pays off in eight months may never break even.

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/prepayment-lifecycle.svg" alt="The Prepayment Lifecycle showing origination through payoff with intervention points" />

What makes this particularly challenging is that prepayment behavior is influenced by forces largely outside the lender's control: movements in prime offer rates, seasonal patterns in home sales, and competitive refinancing offers from other institutions. When rates drop, prepayment activity surges — and it tends to concentrate among the most rate-sensitive, financially sophisticated borrowers.

---

## Understanding the "Why": What Drives Early Payoff

Before building any model, we needed to understand the qualitative drivers of prepayment. We surveyed borrowers who had paid off their loans early and the results were revealing.

The dominant reason for early payoff was **refinancing into a lower-rate product** — either a new home equity loan, a HELOC, or a mortgage refinance that simultaneously paid off the home equity balance. This accounted for roughly 55% of payoff events. The appeal was straightforward: a lower interest rate, often from a competitor.

The second most common driver was **home sales** — approximately 28% of respondents sold their home, which triggered the payoff. Interestingly, many of these borrowers had originally taken the loan for home improvement purposes, suggesting the improvement may have been preparation for selling.

Other, less frequent triggers included debt consolidation through first-lien transactions and life events. But the strategic insight was clear: the primary threat was rate-driven refinancing, and the borrowers most likely to refinance were the ones with the strongest credit profiles — higher income, higher appraisal values, and improving FICO scores. The very attributes that make them attractive acquisition targets also make them the most likely to leave.

---

## Hazard Curve Analysis: When Does Prepayment Risk Peak?

To build an effective detection system, we first needed to understand the *temporal shape* of prepayment risk. We constructed a **hazard curve** — the probability of full payoff in any given month, conditional on the loan having survived to that month.

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/hazard-curve.svg" alt="Prepayment hazard rate curve showing increasing risk over months after origination" />

The hazard curve revealed several important patterns. Prepayment risk was negligible in the first few months after origination — borrowers don't typically pay off a loan they just closed. Risk began to materialize around months four to six, then climbed steadily through the first year. After month twelve, the rate accelerated further, but the data became thinner as fewer loans remained in observation.

This analysis drove a critical design decision: we set our **performance window at twelve months**. The dependent variable for our model became binary — did the loan pay in full within twelve months of origination? This window was long enough to capture meaningful prepayment behavior while retaining enough loans in the dataset to produce statistically robust results. Across the cross-sell portfolio, the twelve-month prepayment rate was approximately 11%.

We also performed **vintage analysis** — tracking prepayment curves by origination quarter. The curves were remarkably consistent across vintages, with one exception: the earliest vintage showed elevated prepayment, attributable to a higher concentration of loans with debt-to-income ratios above 43%. Once underwriting policies tightened to exclude those applicants, subsequent vintages stabilized.

## Building the Detection Engine: Feature Engineering at Scale

The modeling approach was deliberately chosen for interpretability and operational simplicity: a **CHAID-based segmentation tree** (Chi-squared Automatic Interaction Detection). In a regulatory environment where model governance and explainability matter, decision trees offer advantages that black-box approaches cannot — every split is transparent, every node is auditable, and the business logic maps directly to actionable segments.

### Data Assembly

We assembled features from four distinct data domains, each providing a different lens on borrower behavior:

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/feature-engineering.svg" alt="Feature engineering architecture showing four data sources feeding into the segmentation model" />

**Credit Bureau Attributes** formed the largest feature set — trade counts, utilization ratios, inquiry history, age of trades, and balance amounts across bankcard, mortgage, and installment categories. We pulled from two standard bureau file layouts, generating several hundred raw attributes.

**Card Performance Data** captured the borrower's relationship-level behavior — monthly payment amounts, balance trajectories, and critically, the **payment-to-balance ratio** over time. A borrower who consistently pays down their card balance aggressively signals a different risk profile than one who revolves.

**Mortgage and Home Equity Trade Line Data** provided the collateral and first-lien context — balance amounts, terms, payment patterns, lien position, and property value estimates.

**Marketing Attributes** captured the state of the borrower at the time of solicitation — combined loan-to-value, estimated equity, unsecured debt levels, and counts of active loans with balances.

### Temporal Feature Design

One of the most impactful design decisions was the **temporal windowing pattern**. For each raw attribute, we computed not just the current snapshot value, but trailing averages at three-month, six-month, and twelve-month horizons. This transformed a static credit profile into a dynamic signal. For example, a borrower whose card payment-to-balance ratio has been *increasing* over the past six months tells a different story than one whose ratio is flat — the former may be actively deleveraging in preparation for a refinancing event.

The final feature set contained approximately 1,900 candidate variables. The tree algorithm's job was to find the three to four splits that best separated the prepay from non-prepay populations.

## The Segmentation Tree: Anatomy of a Risk Stratification

The segmentation tree's output was elegant in its simplicity. From nearly two thousand candidate features, the algorithm selected a handful of splits that produced six terminal nodes, each with a distinct prepayment risk profile.

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/segmentation-tree.svg" alt="Segmentation tree showing decision splits from root to terminal nodes with prepayment rates" />

The first and most powerful split was on **average age of mortgage trades**. Borrowers with younger mortgage trade lines — meaning they had recently opened or refinanced a mortgage — showed markedly higher prepayment propensity. This makes intuitive sense: someone who recently engaged in mortgage activity is demonstrating comfort with refinancing transactions and is likely still actively shopping rates.

The second split, applied to the younger-mortgage segment, was on **card payment-to-balance ratio** (averaged over three months). Within the already-elevated-risk group, borrowers with lower card utilization — those who weren't carrying large revolving balances — were even more likely to prepay. These are borrowers with financial flexibility; they're not depending on the home equity proceeds to manage other debt.

A third split further refined the highest-risk node by examining the **percentage of trades with high credit utilization**. Borrowers who had very few highly utilized trade lines — in other words, borrowers in strong financial shape across their entire credit profile — represented the peak prepayment risk.

The resulting highest-risk terminal node captured approximately 5% of the total population but contained borrowers with a prepayment rate of ~31% — roughly **2.8 times the base rate**. Combining the top two nodes identified about 10% of the population capturing nearly a quarter of all prepayment events.

### Interpreting the Risk Profile

The segmentation tree confirmed the paradox: the highest prepayment risk concentrates among borrowers who are, by conventional credit metrics, "good" customers. They have younger (recently active) mortgage trade histories, low revolving utilization, and few signs of credit stress. These are financially engaged consumers who are rate-sensitive and have the creditworthiness to obtain competitive offers from competitors.

## Measuring Model Lift: Quantifying Concentration Power

The value of any segmentation model is measured by its **lift** — how much better it identifies the target behavior compared to random selection. We evaluated lift cumulatively, ranking all accounts by their risk node assignment.

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/lift-analysis.svg" alt="Cumulative lift analysis chart showing prepayment capture concentration" />

At the highest-risk node (representing ~5% of the population), the model achieved a cumulative lift of **2.8×** — meaning this segment's prepayment rate was 2.8 times the overall average. Expanding to the top two nodes (~10% of population), the cumulative lift was approximately **2.4×**, capturing roughly 23% of all prepayment events.

We also benchmarked the new tree against the existing segmentation approach, which relied primarily on credit inquiry counts. The incumbent model's top node only represented about 3% of accounts with a 23% prepayment rate (2.1× lift), and its top two nodes delivered just 1.7× cumulative lift. The new tree was a meaningful improvement — better concentration in the top node and more actionable segmentation depth across all nodes.

## Operationalizing the Model: The Two-Pronged Response

A model that scores risk without driving action is an academic exercise. We designed a **two-pronged operational response** that converted risk scores into concrete business actions.

<img src="/images/blog/prepayment-paradox-decision-trees/closed-loop-system.svg" alt="Closed-loop system showing model feeding into acquisition suppression and retention response" />

### Prong 1: Pre-Acquisition Suppression

The primary use case was straightforward: apply the segmentation tree to the solicitation file *before* mailing. Prospects who fell into the highest-risk nodes were suppressed from direct marketing campaigns. The logic is simple — if a borrower has a 31% probability of paying off within twelve months, the expected return on the acquisition investment is significantly diminished.

This required coordination between the analytics team (producing the suppression list), the marketing operations team (applying the filters to campaign files), and the finance team (validating the cost-per-funded-loan impact). The next step was to apply the tree to the most recent campaign file to size the mail volume impact — how many prospects would be suppressed, and what was the expected improvement in portfolio retention.

### Prong 2: Retention Intervention for Existing Borrowers

For borrowers already on the books, we designed a retention response triggered by observable signals — most notably, payoff quote requests. When a servicer reports that a borrower has requested a payoff quote, that is a strong leading indicator of imminent prepayment.

The retention toolkit included three escalating options:

**Retention incentives** — a fixed-dollar rebate or interest credit upon reaching certain account milestones. This is the lightest-touch intervention, requiring no modification to loan terms.

**Rate modification on the existing loan** — reducing the interest rate while keeping all other terms intact, and resetting the monthly payment to match the existing amortization schedule. This skips the underwriting overhead of a new loan and can be executed quickly.

**Pre-qualified refinance into a new product** — offering a new loan at a lower rate, subject to updated borrower and collateral information, following streamlined underwriting procedures. This restarts the amortization schedule and resets the retention clock.

The key constraint is regulatory compliance — any offers made as a result of identifiable trigger events (like a payoff quote request) must comply with fair lending regulations. The retention strategy was designed from the outset with this guardrail in place.

## Key Takeaways for Practitioners

This project reinforced several principles that I carry into every analytics engagement:

**Start with the business problem, not the model.** The most impactful insight wasn't a coefficient or a p-value — it was the business realization that good credit profiles predict bad retention outcomes. Framing the problem correctly matters more than algorithmic sophistication.

**Interpretability is a feature, not a constraint.** We deliberately chose a segmentation tree over more complex ensemble methods because the operational stakeholders needed to understand, audit, and explain every split. In regulated industries, a model you can defend is worth more than one that ekes out an extra percentage point of AUC.

**Temporal feature engineering is where the signal lives.** The trailing-average windowing pattern — computing three-month, six-month, and twelve-month moving averages of raw attributes — was the single most impactful feature design decision. Static snapshots miss the behavioral trends that precede prepayment events.

**Vintage analysis is non-negotiable.** Without tracking performance by origination cohort, you cannot distinguish between model drift, portfolio composition changes, and genuine shifts in borrower behavior. Every lending model should have vintage-level monitoring built in from day one.

**Close the loop.** A model that scores but doesn't act is a cost center. The value was realized not in the prediction itself, but in the two operational responses it enabled: suppression that reduced wasted acquisition spend, and retention offers that preserved revenue from at-risk accounts.
