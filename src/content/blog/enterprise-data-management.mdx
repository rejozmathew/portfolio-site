### The Pile of Bikes

Imagine you lead a team of engineers and scientists. Your organization's leadership has just announced an ambitious goal: colonize Mars within the decade. You'll need spaceships — purpose-built vehicles capable of navigating complex environments, carrying specialized payloads, and operating with precision and reliability.

Now imagine you walk into the hangar where your organization keeps its aerospace inventory. Instead of spaceships, you find a warehouse full of bicycles. Thousands of them. Some assembled, some in pieces. No labels, no inventory list, no quality inspection records. Nobody can tell you which parts are compatible, which are broken, or even how many there are.

That is, roughly speaking, what the data landscape looks like inside most large enterprises. There is no shortage of data — in a typical enterprise data environment, you might find hundreds of thousands of tables and tens of millions of attributes. But there is a profound shortage of *organization*. No comprehensive catalogue. No consistent quality assurance. No glossary that connects technical column names to business meaning. The data exists, but nobody can reliably find it, trust it, or build on top of it.

This article is about what it takes to turn that pile of bikes into a launchpad. Not the spaceships themselves — I've written separately about the [marketing analytical database](/blog/marketing-analytical-database), the [feature store](/blog/feature-store-foundation), the [identity graph](/blog/identity-graph), the [semantic layer](/blog/semantic-layer), the [MLOps platform](/blog/enterprise-mlops), and the [domain-centric data architecture](/blog/enterprise-data-modernization) that we built as next-generation data capabilities. This article is about the foundational data management work that made all of those possible — and that most organizations underestimate by an order of magnitude.

### Why Data Management Is a Strategic Initiative, Not a Side Project

When organizations think about data, the conversation almost always gravitates toward the exciting outcomes: machine learning models, real-time dashboards, personalization engines, GenAI-powered assistants. These are the spaceships. They capture executive attention and investor interest.

But the outcomes are downstream consumers. They amplify whatever quality — or lack thereof — exists in the underlying data. And the underlying data, in most enterprises, is in rough shape.

The case for data management as a strategic initiative rests on three pillars, and only one of them involves AI.

**Regulatory and controls.** In regulated industries — financial services, healthcare, insurance — you are required to demonstrate that your data is accurate, traceable, and governed. Regulatory frameworks like FCRA, ECOA, and CFPB consent orders don't merely ask whether your customer communications are correct; they ask whether you can *prove* they're correct, whether you can trace the data lineage from source to output, and whether you have controls in place to detect when something goes wrong. I've written about the [operating model for controls monitoring and incident management](/blog/bfsi-controls-monitoring) that we built — but that operating model is only as good as the data foundation underneath it. If you don't know what data you have, where it lives, and what it means, you cannot build effective monitoring controls around it. SOX auditors don't ask whether your data is interesting; they ask whether your data is controlled.

**Business growth.** Marketing attribution, customer analytics, personalization, and operational reporting — these are the day-to-day workhorses of a data-driven organization. They require curated, trustworthy data assets with clear definitions and consistent calculations. Every time an analyst spends two hours hunting for the right table, or two teams arrive at different numbers for the same metric because they pulled from different sources, that's a direct tax on business velocity. The [semantic layer](/blog/semantic-layer) we built addressed the consistency problem — but the semantic layer itself had to be built on top of catalogued, quality-validated domain structures.

**Advanced analytics and AI.** Yes, ML models need good data. Feature stores need reliable feature pipelines. GenAI applications need curated document collections. But framing data management as an "AI prerequisite" sells it short. Data management is an enterprise prerequisite. AI is one of many consumers.

In our case, data management was elevated to a strategic workstream in the consumer banking organization's multi-year plan — at the same level as revenue initiatives and technology modernization. It was part of the president of consumer banking's strategic agenda. That level of executive sponsorship is what made a five-year effort viable. Without it, data management becomes a "boil the ocean" aspiration that dies on the vine when the first quarterly budget review arrives.

### The Three Mandates

When we stood up a dedicated data management function, we organized the work around three mandates. I want to be clear: these reflect what *our* organization needed given its data priorities and starting point. Another organization might slice the work differently — a company with mature cataloguing but weak analytics infrastructure would emphasize different mandates than one sitting on a decade of undocumented tables. The framework is illustrative, not prescriptive.

<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 2: How we organized our data management function — your organization's mandates will reflect its own data priorities and starting point.
</span>

**Mandate 1: Design and build next-generation data assets.** This is the work of creating the curated, domain-centric data structures that replace the ad-hoc landscape. The [marketing analytical database](/blog/marketing-analytical-database), the [feature store](/blog/feature-store-foundation), the [identity graph](/blog/identity-graph), and the broader [data modernization architecture](/blog/enterprise-data-modernization) all fall under this mandate. These are the "spaceships" — purpose-built for specific analytical and operational missions. They are covered in depth in their respective articles.

**Mandate 2: BAU data operations.** In a large consumer banking organization, each business unit — deposits, personal loans, credit cards, and so on — has its own stream of ongoing data work. A pricing grid changes and two new attributes need to be pulled from the operational system. A product feature launches and requires new data flows to support reporting and analytics. A source system upgrade introduces schema changes that ripple through downstream tables. This is the day-to-day work of data product management: prioritizing requests with business stakeholders, performing business and data analysis, coordinating with data engineering to implement changes, and testing and validating the results. It doesn't generate the same kind of visibility as building a new analytical platform, but it represents a significant portion of the team's capacity and directly impacts the business units' ability to operate.

**Mandate 3: Catalogue, steward, and govern existing data assets.** Before you can build anything new, you need to understand what you have. This means inventorying existing datasets, enriching them with business context, profiling them for quality, establishing stewardship accountability, and maintaining ongoing governance as the landscape evolves. This is the most labor-intensive and least glamorous of the three mandates — and it's the one this article focuses on. Importantly, the governance and stewardship practices established here aren't confined to this mandate. They are cross-cutting disciplines that apply to Mandates 1 and 2 as well — any data work, whether it's building a new analytical platform or processing a routine product change, needs to follow the same cataloguing and quality standards.

The rest of this article focuses on Mandate 3 — the cataloguing and stewardship work — because it is the part of data management that practitioners most often underestimate, and the part least often written about in depth.

### The Inventory Problem

The first step in any data management program is deceptively simple to state and painfully difficult to execute: figure out what you have.

In a typical enterprise data warehouse, the scope is staggering. In our environment, the enterprise contained upwards of 500,000 tables and 23 million attributes across all platforms. Within the consumer banking organization alone, the known subset comprised approximately 25,000 tables. Most of these had no business descriptions, no documented ownership, no quality metrics, and no lineage metadata. They existed as technical objects — DDL definitions with column names like `CMPGN_SUB_CTGY_CDE` and `FST_LOAN_INT_RATE` that meant something to the person who created them years ago but were opaque to everyone else.

The inventory problem has two dimensions. The first is *enumeration*: simply creating a list of what exists, where it lives, and what system created it. This is partially automatable — metadata harvesting tools can crawl your data warehouse and produce a technical inventory of tables, columns, data types, and row counts. The second dimension is *understanding*: determining what each asset actually means, who uses it, what business processes it supports, and how it relates to other assets. This dimension is fundamentally human work. No crawler can tell you that `V_CLB_BASIC.BNK_LOAN_ACCT` is the primary view for personal lending account attributes, that it's refreshed daily from the servicing platform, and that the `INIT_LOAN_AMT` field represents the original funded amount at origination rather than the approved credit limit. That knowledge lives in people's heads, and the job of cataloguing is to get it out of their heads and into a system.

We learned early that trying to catalogue everything at once is a recipe for paralysis. 25,000 tables is not a cataloguing project — it's a cataloguing career. You need a triage strategy.

### Prioritization and Stratification

We adopted a stratified approach based on three factors: business criticality, regulatory exposure, and downstream consumption volume. Every dataset was classified into one of three priority tiers — high, medium, or low — and the cataloguing work was sequenced accordingly.

High-priority datasets were those that fed regulatory reporting, supported active analytical models, or served as foundational reference data used across multiple business lines. Medium-priority datasets included operational reporting tables, secondary analytical assets, and data structures that supported specific business functions but weren't cross-cutting. Low-priority datasets covered archival structures, deprecated tables still lingering in the environment, and single-use analytical outputs. The total: over 1,000 consumer banking datasets that needed to be brought under management, excluding enterprise-wide tables that fell under a broader organizational effort.

The sequencing was realistic, not aspirational. We allocated approximately 20 percent of each team member's time — roughly eight hours per week — to cataloguing activities. Based on what we observed, each attribute required about six minutes to fully catalogue when you factored in the research, the conversations with subject matter experts, the documentation, and the profiling analysis. For a table with 30 attributes, that's three hours of focused work just for the attribute-level metadata, before you account for dataset-level documentation, lineage mapping, and quality rule definition.

The timeline we planned was measured in quarters: high-priority datasets by end of Q2, a substantial portion of medium-priority by end of Q3, remaining medium and a portion of low-priority by end of Q4, and the remainder stretching into the following year. In practice, it stretched beyond that — competing enterprise initiatives, resource constraints, and the natural scope expansion that comes from discovering new complexity as you catalogue created delays. This is normal. Data management at scale is a multi-year commitment, and any plan that pretends otherwise is setting itself up for failure.

### The Cataloguing Framework

The cataloguing framework is the template that defines what metadata gets captured for each asset. Getting this right matters: too sparse, and the catalogue becomes a glorified list of table names that nobody consults; too elaborate, and the cataloguing effort bogs down in documentation overhead that produces diminishing returns.

We designed a two-level framework — dataset-level metadata and attribute-level metadata — that balanced completeness with practicality.

<img src="/images/blog/enterprise-data-management/cataloguing-framework.svg" alt="Data cataloguing framework showing dataset-level and attribute-level metadata fields" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 3: The two-level cataloguing framework — dataset-level metadata captures the "what and why" of a data asset, while attribute-level metadata captures the "what exactly" of each column.
</span>

**Dataset-level metadata** captures the identity and context of the data asset as a whole. This includes the dataset name and a plain-language business description (what this data represents and why it exists), the domain area it belongs to (accounts, customers, transactions, products, marketing, etc.), the source system and extraction method, refresh frequency, the assigned data steward, lineage context (upstream sources and known downstream consumers), current record count, priority tier, and sample queries that illustrate common analytical access patterns. The sample queries were a late addition to our framework, but they turned out to be one of the most valued elements — analysts new to a dataset could see immediately how their predecessors had used it.

**Attribute-level metadata** is where the depth lives. For each column in a catalogued dataset, we captured the attribute name, a business definition in plain language (not just a restatement of the column name), the data type and precision, PII and sensitivity flags (PII, PCI, PHI, and any internal classification markers), domain tags for thematic grouping, the attribute's position in any domain hierarchy (e.g., Account → Loan → Origination), profiling highlights from the most recent data profile (null rate, min/max/average, unique count, duplicate percentage, skew), associated data quality rules, and free-form notes for anything that required human judgment — outlier explanations, known quirks, pending investigation items.

The PII and sensitivity flagging deserves special attention. In a regulated environment, knowing which attributes contain personally identifiable information isn't just a nice-to-have — it's a compliance requirement. Privacy regulations (CCPA, GLBA), data protection standards (PCI DSS), and internal data classification policies all require organizations to know where sensitive data resides. Flagging PII at the attribute level in the catalogue creates a single source of truth for data protection teams, access control policies, and incident response processes.

The distinction between **technical metadata** and **business metadata** is important to understand, because it determines where automation helps and where it doesn't. Technical metadata — column names, data types, row counts, null rates, value distributions — can be harvested automatically through profiling tools. We built an Excel-based profiling tool early on that could connect to our data warehouse, run statistical analyses on every column in a table, and produce a structured output showing completeness, cardinality, distribution characteristics, and type validation. This was the fast part. Business metadata — the plain-language definitions, the domain classifications, the lineage context, the "why does this column have 48% nulls and is that expected" annotations — required human judgment. A profiling tool can tell you that `ACCT_CLSE_DT` has a 48% null rate; only a human who understands the business can tell you that this is expected because 48% of accounts in the portfolio are still open and haven't closed yet.

The catalogued metadata was loaded into a data catalogue platform that provided a searchable, browsable interface for the broader organization. The goal was to create an experience analogous to an internal search engine for data — analysts could search for concepts (e.g., "loan origination amount") rather than needing to know specific table and column names. The catalogue integrated with our data quality monitoring so that when a dataset had active quality issues, that information surfaced directly in the catalogue entry, warning consumers that the data might not be trustworthy at that moment.

### Data Profiling as a Discovery and Validation Tool

Data profiling is the act of systematically analyzing the contents of a dataset to understand its structure, content, and quality characteristics. It's the diagnostic step — the equivalent of running a comprehensive physical exam on your data before prescribing any treatment.

A thorough data profile for a given table produces column-level statistics that include: null and completeness rates (what percentage of rows have values for each column), minimum, maximum, and average values for numeric and date fields, skew (whether the distribution is symmetric or heavily tailed), unique value counts and cardinality (how many distinct values exist relative to the total row count), duplicate percentage (how much redundancy exists), and data type validation (whether the actual values conform to the declared data type).

What makes profiling valuable isn't the statistics themselves — it's the questions they surface. Consider a few examples from our experience. A column declared as an integer key has 3,586,246 rows and 3,586,246 unique values — that's a legitimate primary key with no duplicates, which confirms the grain of the table. But an ID column with 3,446,386 unique values out of 3,586,246 rows suggests that roughly 4% of records share an ID with another record — is that a data quality issue, or is it expected because the table contains historical snapshots of the same entity? A numeric amount field with a minimum of 10 and a maximum of 1,674,400 but an average of 14,313 and a skew of 3 tells you the distribution has a long right tail — most values are modest, but there are outliers in the hundreds of thousands or millions that may warrant investigation. A date field with a 48% null rate needs context: is this a close date that's null for open accounts, or is it a required field with a data quality problem?

These are the kinds of questions that profiling puts on the table. Without profiling, these issues remain invisible — buried in the data, silently corrupting downstream analyses until someone notices a number that doesn't look right and spends days tracing it back to the source.

We ran profiling as a systematic activity during the cataloguing effort. Every dataset that was catalogued received a full profile, and the profiling highlights were captured as part of the attribute-level metadata. This created a baseline — a snapshot of what the data looked like at a point in time — that we could then use to define quality expectations and detect when behavior changed.

### Data Quality Rules and Anomaly Detection

Profiling gives you a snapshot. Quality rules give you ongoing assurance. The transition from profiling to continuous quality monitoring is the step that moves data management from a project (something you do once) to an operating capability (something that runs continuously).

<img src="/images/blog/enterprise-data-management/profiling-to-monitoring.svg" alt="Four-phase lifecycle from data profiling through enrichment, quality rules, and continuous monitoring" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 4: The lifecycle from point-in-time profiling to continuous quality assurance — each phase feeds metadata back into the catalogue.
</span>

Data quality rules codify expectations about what the data *should* look like, derived from what the profiling and business context told you it *does* look like under normal conditions. The standard taxonomy of quality dimensions — widely recognized in data management frameworks like DAMA-DMBOK and tested by certifications like CDMP — provides a useful organizing structure.

**Completeness** checks validate that required fields are populated. If `BNK_ACCT_KEY` should never be null (because it's the primary key), that's a completeness rule. If `INIT_LOAN_AMT` should be populated for all active loan accounts, that's a conditional completeness rule.

**Validity** constraints enforce that values fall within expected ranges or belong to expected reference sets. A loan amount should be positive and below a reasonable upper bound. A product code should match a known set of valid codes. A date should not be in the future for a historical close date.

**Consistency** rules check that related values across columns or across tables agree with each other. An account close date should not precede an account open date. An aggregate calculated from transaction-level data should match the pre-aggregated value stored in a summary table.

**Timeliness** and freshness checks monitor whether data is arriving on schedule. If a table is expected to refresh daily by 6 AM, a freshness rule alerts when the data is stale. Late-arriving data can cascade through downstream pipelines and reports, creating silent failures that are far more dangerous than a loud error.

**Uniqueness** assertions validate grain constraints — ensuring that what should be a primary key actually is, and that duplicate records haven't been introduced by ETL errors or source system issues.

Beyond rule-based checks, we implemented an anomaly detection layer that used statistical methods to identify unexpected changes in data behavior. Where a rule says "this column should never be null," anomaly detection says "this column's null rate has historically been 0.1% — today it's 12%, and that deviation is statistically significant." This is a fundamentally different capability: rules encode known expectations, while anomaly detection surfaces *unknown* issues by learning normal patterns and flagging departures.

The anomaly detection approach uses unsupervised machine learning to build a model of each monitored table's normal behavior across multiple dimensions — row counts, value distributions, null rates, freshness, categorical distributions, and numeric ranges. When the data deviates from the learned pattern, the system flags it, provides a severity assessment, and generates root cause analysis that points to the likely source of the problem — a specific column, a specific partition, or a specific time window where behavior changed. This is particularly valuable in high-volume environments where the number of tables and attributes under management makes manual inspection infeasible. You cannot have a human reviewing profiling statistics for 1,000+ tables every morning. You need a system that watches continuously and alerts selectively.

The integration between quality monitoring and the data catalogue is critical. When an anomaly is detected on a catalogued dataset, that information should surface directly in the catalogue entry. Consumers browsing the catalogue for data to use in an analysis should see, right alongside the business description and profiling statistics, whether the dataset currently has active quality issues. This creates a "trust signal" — a real-time indicator of whether the data is fit for use at this moment, not just whether it was fit when it was last manually reviewed.

Quality monitoring also connects directly to the broader controls and incident management framework. A data quality failure — a table that didn't refresh on time, a column that suddenly has an unexpected null rate spike, a referential integrity violation between related tables — can be a control failure with regulatory implications. The ability to detect these issues automatically, triage them through a structured incident management process, and produce governance-ready reporting on time-to-detection and time-to-resolution is what turns data quality from a technical housekeeping function into an enterprise risk management capability.

### The Stewardship Operating Model

Cataloguing and quality monitoring produce metadata and alerts. Stewardship determines who *acts* on them.

A stewardship operating model defines three distinct roles. **Data owners** are business leaders who have accountability for a data domain — they make decisions about what data should be collected, how it should be used, and what quality standards are acceptable. **Data stewards** are the operational counterparts — typically senior analysts or data management professionals who are responsible for the day-to-day care and feeding of data assets within their assigned domain. They review quality alerts, maintain business definitions, approve access requests, and serve as the point of contact when downstream consumers have questions about the data. **Technical custodians** — usually data engineers or database administrators — manage the physical infrastructure: storage, ETL pipelines, access controls, and performance optimization.

In our model, every catalogued dataset had an assigned data steward. Stewardship assignments were organized by domain area — an individual or small team would own all datasets within a given domain (e.g., all account-related tables, or all transaction-related tables), rather than having stewardship fragmented across unrelated assets. This domain-aligned model created depth of expertise: the steward for account data understood the business context, the source system behavior, and the downstream consumption patterns well enough to make informed judgments when quality issues arose.

The stewardship model also defined how the governance practices applied to ongoing BAU data work — the day-to-day changes driven by each business unit. When a pricing grid changed and required new attributes from an operational system, or when a product launch needed new data flows, the data management team was engaged early in the process. We participated in value stream and Agile workflows as a consultative function, helping project teams think through how new data would fit into the existing ecosystem, what cataloguing and quality requirements applied, and whether the change introduced dependencies or conflicts with existing assets. This "govern at creation" approach — applying Mandate 3's stewardship discipline to the Mandate 2 operational workstream — was far more effective than trying to retroactively catalogue and quality-check data that had already been built and deployed. Even a routine two-attribute addition deserved the same documentation rigor as a net-new analytical platform.

### The Capabilities This Foundation Unlocks

The cataloguing, profiling, quality monitoring, and stewardship work described above took years — not months — to build to maturity. It was painstaking, frequently unglamorous, and constantly competing for resources with more visible initiatives. But it was the prerequisite for everything that came after.

With a catalogued, quality-monitored data environment and an operating stewardship model, we were able to build a series of next-generation data capabilities that would not have been trustworthy or scalable without the foundation:

The **[enterprise data modernization architecture](/blog/enterprise-data-modernization)** — a domain-centric, four-layer framework that transformed raw source data into curated, consumable, business-friendly structures — relied on the cataloguing work to identify which source assets existed, what they contained, and how they related to each other. Without an inventory, you cannot design domain structures; you're building in the dark.

The **[marketing analytical database](/blog/marketing-analytical-database)** — a curated set of business process data assets purpose-built for marketing automation, analytics, and reporting — required understanding the lineage and quality characteristics of the source data it consumed. The catalogue and quality baseline told us which sources were trustworthy enough to feed a marketing-critical asset.

The **[feature store and customer data infrastructure](/blog/feature-store-foundation)** — an entity-centric framework for feature engineering and ML model development — needed reliable, well-understood data to produce consistent, reproducible features. A feature computed from an uncatalogued, unmonitored source table is a feature you cannot trust in production.

The **[identity graph](/blog/identity-graph)** — resolving customer identity across marketing, fraud, and personalization use cases — was the final and most complex capability we delivered. It required the deepest data foundation: comprehensive understanding of identity-related attributes across multiple systems, rigorous quality standards for matching logic, and clear lineage from source records to resolved entities. This was the last piece of the five-year journey.

The **[semantic layer](/blog/semantic-layer)** — centralizing KPI calculations and providing self-service BI access — depended on standardized definitions and consistent source data. The business definitions captured during cataloguing became the foundation for semantic layer metric definitions.

The **[MLOps platform](/blog/enterprise-mlops)** — the infrastructure for model development, deployment, and monitoring — required governed data pipelines and quality assurance at the data layer. Our team's contribution here was primarily from a product requirements standpoint, specifying what the data management integration points needed to look like.

The **[controls monitoring and incident management operating model](/blog/bfsi-controls-monitoring)** — the closed-loop system for regulatory control execution, monitoring, incident triage, and remediation — required knowing what data existed, what quality expectations applied, and who was accountable when something went wrong. The stewardship model and quality monitoring infrastructure we built became integral components of the broader control environment.

Each of these capabilities is documented in its own article. What connects them is that none of them would have been built on a chaotic, uncatalogued, unmonitored data environment. The foundation came first.

### The Five-Year Arc

Data management maturity doesn't happen in a sprint cycle. It is a multi-year journey with distinct phases, and understanding that arc helps set realistic expectations for organizations embarking on similar efforts.

<img src="/images/blog/enterprise-data-management/maturity-progression.svg" alt="Four-stage data management maturity progression from chaotic through aware, managed, and governed" />
<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 5: The maturity arc — most enterprises begin at Stage 1. Getting to Stage 4 requires sustained investment, executive sponsorship, and honest acceptance that this is a multi-year commitment.
</span>

**Stage 1 — Chaotic.** No inventory of data assets. Knowledge about the data lives exclusively in the heads of long-tenured individuals. Quality issues are discovered reactively — usually when a report produces a wrong number and someone traces it back to the source. There is no stewardship model, no cataloguing standard, and no quality monitoring. In our case, this was the starting point: 500,000+ tables across the enterprise, no catalogue, no organization.

**Stage 2 — Aware.** The organization recognizes the problem and stands up a dedicated function. Inventory and prioritization begin. Profiling tools are built or procured. High-priority datasets get catalogued first. Stewardship roles are defined and assignments are made. This phase is characterized by heavy manual effort and a growing understanding of just how much work remains. For us, this encompassed roughly the first two years.

**Stage 3 — Managed.** Data quality rules are in production. Anomaly detection is actively monitoring critical datasets. The catalogue is broadly adopted — analysts know to consult it, and it reflects current reality rather than a stale snapshot from two years ago. New data entering the environment follows the governance process. The organization begins building next-generation data assets (marketing analytical database, customer data infrastructure, domain-centric structures) with confidence because the foundation is solid enough to build on. This phase covered approximately years two through four.

**Stage 4 — Governed.** Proactive monitoring is the norm — issues are detected and triaged before consumers notice them. Self-service data discovery is possible through the catalogue. Control gates are enforced in project execution workflows. The data environment is ready for advanced capabilities: ML feature engineering, identity resolution, GenAI pipelines. The foundation is strong enough that the organization can focus on innovation rather than firefighting. For us, the identity graph work — the final major capability — was delivered around year five.

The arc was not smooth. Along the way, we dealt with resource constraints (the team was also responsible for regulatory reporting and BI analytics, so cataloguing competed with day-to-day operational demands), scope changes (the definition of "catalogued" expanded as we learned more about what metadata was actually needed), tooling limitations (early profiling tools needed to evolve as the scale of the effort grew), and the constant gravitational pull of more visible, more exciting projects. The project plans from this era show a mix of green, yellow, and red status indicators — because this is what real enterprise programs look like.

### The Honest Truth About Data Management

There is a persistent myth in enterprise data work that the "real" value is in the models, the dashboards, and the AI applications. The data management layer — the cataloguing, the profiling, the quality rules, the stewardship assignments — is treated as overhead. Something you do because governance requires it, not because it creates value.

That framing is exactly backwards. The models, dashboards, and AI applications are *expressions* of data value. The data management layer is what makes that value *trustworthy*. A machine learning model trained on uncatalogued, unmonitored data is a liability, not an asset. A regulatory report generated from a source table with an undetected quality issue is a compliance risk. A marketing campaign targeted using a customer attribute with a 30% error rate is money set on fire.

The work described in this article — standing up a data management function, cataloguing hundreds of datasets, profiling millions of attributes, implementing quality rules and anomaly detection, building a stewardship operating model — took five years and required sustained executive sponsorship. It was not the kind of work that generates conference keynotes or LinkedIn engagement. But it was the work that made the spaceships possible.

If your organization is sitting on a pile of bikes and wondering why it can't get to Mars, this is where you start.
