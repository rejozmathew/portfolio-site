### The Problem No One Plans For

When an organization decides to wind down a line of business and transfer its portfolio to another servicer, the data migration playbook is well understood: extract account records, transform them to the receiving platform's format, validate, and ship. Teams plan for it. Vendors have tools for it. It's hard, but it's tractable.

What nobody plans for is the documents.

Not the structured data — the actual *documents*. The promissory notes, the cosigner addendums, the final disclosures, the tax forms, the statements, the correspondence. The physical and digital artifacts that prove a borrower agreed to specific terms, that a cosigner accepted liability, that a disclosure was delivered on a particular date. In a consumer lending business, these documents aren't nice-to-haves. They're legally required evidence of the lending relationship — and when completeness expectations accompany a portfolio sale, "we think we have most of them somewhere" is not an adequate position.

We faced exactly this situation. Our organization was winding down its student lending operation and transferring the full portfolio — roughly two million borrower accounts — to a new servicer. The account data transfer was complex but conventional. The document transfer was something else entirely: approximately 200 million documents, scattered across five different source systems, accumulated over more than 25 years, with metadata that ranged from pristine to nonexistent. The transfer required near-complete document delivery — not as a best-effort target, but as an expectation tied to the portfolio sale. Priority documents — promissory notes, disclosures, addendums — had to arrive on the *day* of each transfer tranche. Everything else within 45 days.

This article walks through the architecture we built to solve that problem: how we centralized fragmented metadata, built a three-tier matching strategy that deliberately exhausted cheap methods before deploying expensive ones, and used a RAG pipeline — embedding-based semantic search with LLM extraction and explanation generation — to resolve the residual that structured approaches couldn't reach, with human-in-the-loop validation ensuring that every AI-assisted match decision had governance behind it.

### The Fragmented Source Landscape

Our documents lived in five fundamentally different systems. The loan origination system stored application-stage documents in a relational database, indexed by application ID. The servicing platform generated statements automatically and stored them in an enterprise archive with a proprietary format. A content management system held inbound customer mail and agent-uploaded documents with free-form categorization. An external print vendor retained outbound disclosures as daily bulk PDF files with minimal indexing. And an enterprise document vault stored servicing-triggered letters in a date-batched archive format that required API-based retrieval. Each system had its own identifier scheme, its own categorization taxonomy, its own storage format, and its own gaps.

Over 25 years, the way documents were tagged, categorized, and associated with accounts changed multiple times. Early-vintage loans had documents with borrower ID only — no loan-level association. Mid-vintage loans introduced application IDs but with inconsistent formats. Some systems used free-text document categories; others used structured codes. A document tagged as "Application - Promissory Note" in one system might appear as "PNote" in another, or as "Other Servicing Document" in a third because an agent selected the wrong category during manual upload. These weren't edge cases — they represented a meaningful portion of the total population.

### The Central Metadata Repository

The central metadata repository was the single most important architectural decision in the entire program. Without it, gap analysis would have required querying five separate systems in real-time — a logistical impossibility given the volumes and security constraints involved.

The repository lived in Snowflake as a set of structured tables. The core table held one row per document-account association, with fields for: customer identifier (CIF), loan identifier, application identifier, document type (normalized), document source system, document creation date, physical storage location (a pointer, not the document itself), and a match confidence indicator.

Populating this repository required coordination across five source teams, each of which had to extract their metadata, map it to a standardized format, and push it through a secure file transfer pipeline into the warehouse. The key challenge was normalization — dates came in different formats, document type taxonomies didn't align, and some systems used surrogate keys that had to be mapped back to the master account identifier. We invested significant effort in building a translation layer that harmonized these differences before loading. Our team then ran the standardization, deduplication, and matching processes.

The structure of the metadata that would eventually be shared with the receiving servicer followed a defined specification: borrower identifiers, name fields, document type code, loan identifier, date received, sale/transfer date, a deal identifier, and the physical file name. Building our central repository with this output format in mind meant we could generate transfer-ready files directly from the warehouse without an additional transformation step.

### The Cost-Management Insight: Three Tiers of Resolution

The core technical problem was this: given a document with some metadata, determine which of the two million accounts it belongs to. When the metadata was rich and consistent, this was straightforward. When it wasn't, we needed progressively more sophisticated — and more expensive — approaches.

We designed a three-tier resolution strategy, and the design was as much about *cost and complexity management* as it was about accuracy. Deterministic matching is essentially free (SQL joins). Probabilistic matching is computationally cheap (scoring functions). The RAG pipeline is expensive per document (embedding generation, vector search, LLM inference). By reserving the most expensive approach for the residual that cheaper methods couldn't resolve, we kept the AI footprint small enough to be practical — both in compute cost and in the human review effort required to validate results. In our case, this cascade reduced the GenAI scope by roughly 30×.

<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>

**Tier 1: Deterministic Matching.** The first pass used exact-key joins. If a document's metadata contained a customer identifier (CIF) or application ID that mapped directly to an account in the master table, the match was automatic. This resolved roughly 85% of the total population. The high match rate was driven primarily by the servicing platform and document vault, which had well-structured metadata with reliable identifiers. The origination system and content management system had significantly lower deterministic match rates, particularly for older vintages.

**Tier 2: Probabilistic Matching.** For the ~15% that didn't match deterministically, we built a probabilistic scoring engine. This used a weighted combination of partial identifiers: last four digits of SSN, borrower name similarity (Jaro-Winkler distance), document creation date proximity to known account events (origination date, first payment date), and document type heuristics (e.g., a cosigner addendum should only match to accounts that had a cosigner). Each signal contributed to a composite confidence score between 0 and 100. Matches scoring above 80 were accepted automatically; matches between 50 and 80 were flagged for review. This tier resolved approximately 12% of the remaining population, bringing the cumulative match rate to approximately 97%.

**Tier 3: The RAG Pipeline.** The remaining ~3% was the hard residual — documents with severely incomplete metadata, mismatched identifiers, or content from legacy systems where the tagging was unreliable. Critically, many of these documents were scanned images or legacy PDFs where *the only identifying information existed in the document text itself*, not in the structured metadata. A promissory note from 2003 might have no CIF, no application ID, and an incorrect document category — but the text of the document contained a borrower name and a date that could be matched to an account if you could *read* it. This is the class of problem where traditional record linkage approaches, no matter how sophisticated, reach their ceiling — and where semantic understanding of document content becomes genuinely necessary.

### The Tier 3 RAG Pipeline: Retrieve, Augment, Generate

Every Tier 3 document runs through the same pipeline: OCR extraction, LLM-assisted field extraction, PII tokenization, embedding, vector retrieval, and finally LLM-assisted matching with explanation generation. The pipeline follows the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pattern: top candidate accounts are *retrieved* from the vector index, those candidates are passed as context that *augments* the LLM prompt alongside the document metadata, and the LLM *generates* a match decision, a confidence score, and a natural-language explanation. The explanation is generated for *every* document — regardless of confidence — because it serves the audit trail.

High-confidence matches are auto-accepted; everything else goes to a human review queue. Both paths write to an immutable audit trail.

**OCR and LLM Extraction.** Documents in S3 were processed through Amazon Textract, which handled scanned PDFs, faxed documents, and form images. But raw OCR gives you a wall of text — it doesn't know which "date" is the origination date versus a date of birth versus a notary date, or which "name" is the borrower versus the cosigner versus a bank officer. And the Tier 3 residual isn't just clean promissory notes with labeled fields. It's a mix of scanned driver's licenses submitted as identity verification, W-9 tax forms, cosigner addendums with two names, faxed correspondence from 2004 with degraded scan quality, statements with account numbers buried in headers. Each has identifying information, but in completely different formats and contexts.

This is why the LLM extraction step is essential — and why it needs to be a capable model. We used AWS Bedrock (the same VPC-isolated deployment pattern we'd established for other AI workloads) with a prompt tailored to the document context: "This is a scanned document associated with a lending portfolio. Extract: the most likely borrower name, any government identifier digits, any dates that could indicate loan origination, any account or loan identifiers, and the document type. The document may be a supporting document (ID verification, tax form) rather than a loan document." The LLM understands that "DL Name: SMITH, JAMES A" on a driver's license is a borrower name, that "March 10, 2015" next to "Disbursement Date" is an origination date while "June 15, 1992" is a date of birth, and that "Acct: 48291037" on a statement header is an account number. It normalizes heterogeneous documents to a consistent extracted schema.

**PII Tokenization.** Critical identifiers — government IDs, dates of birth, account numbers — were tokenized through the enterprise data protection platform using format-preserving encryption before reaching the cloud. A tokenized SSN is still 9 digits, just different digits — and crucially, the *same* raw value always produces the *same* token. Both the account metadata in Snowflake and the LLM-extracted document fields passed through the same tokenization instance, producing identical tokens for identical values. Names, dates (non-DOB), and document attributes flowed in clear text, giving the embedding model the semantic signal it needed. The matching pipeline operated on a mix of clear-text fields for semantic search and consistently tokenized fields for deterministic confirmation — without ever exposing raw sensitive identifiers in the cloud.

**Extraction Validation.** Before embedding, we cross-checked the LLM-extracted fields against whatever source metadata was available. If the document's source system said "created: 2015-03-12" but the LLM extracted "date: 2008-11-20" from the body, that conflict was flagged. Documents with extraction conflicts were routed directly to the human review queue with an "extraction mismatch" indicator — the reviewer saw both the source metadata and the LLM's extraction and adjudicated. This catches extraction errors *before* they enter the embedding step and produce silent false matches downstream.

**Text Assembly and Embeddings.** The embedding model doesn't compare database fields — it compares *text*. For each account, we assembled a labeled text block from the identity fields available: `Borrower: James A. Smith / SSN: 847-29-3651 (tokenized) / DOB: 293-84-1567 (tokenized) / Account: 7821-4039 (tokenized) / Origination: 2015-03-10 / Program: Undergraduate / Cosigner: Maria Smith`. Not every account has a cosigner; not every document will match on every field. The field labels serve as semantic anchors — "Borrower:" tells the model that what follows is a person's name, enabling it to recognize that "Borrower: James Smith" in the account index and "J. Smith" in the OCR extraction are references to the same person. For documents, the LLM-extracted fields were assembled using the same labeling pattern, creating embeddings in the same vector space.

**How partial information works.** A critical property of embedding-based search is that it degrades gracefully with sparse input. A promissory note with name, date, SSN, and account number produces a rich embedding that scores very high against the correct account (~0.90+ cosine similarity). A driver's license with only a name and tokenized DOB produces a sparser embedding — it still pulls the right account into the top-K candidates, but with a lower score (~0.55-0.70), and possibly with other accounts named "James Smith" scoring similarly. The embedding search isn't making the match decision — it's narrowing two million accounts down to five plausible candidates. The LLM step evaluates those candidates, and the confidence score reflects how much signal was available. Documents with fewer matching fields naturally produce lower confidence, which routes them to human review. The system self-corrects.

**The Retrieval and Generation Steps.** Each account's labeled text was embedded via Bedrock and stored as a native vector column in Snowflake, queried using Snowflake's built-in cosine similarity function — no separate vector database infrastructure needed. For each document, the tokenized, validated extraction was embedded using the same model and searched against the Snowflake vector index to retrieve the top-K candidates. Those candidates were passed back to Bedrock in a matching prompt. The LLM produced three outputs: a match decision, a confidence score, and a natural-language explanation articulating the evidence.

**Document-Type-Aware Confidence Scoring.** Not every document carries the same fields — and the confidence scoring needed to reflect that. A driver's license with a perfect name and DOB match is a strong result: those are the *only* identifying fields that document type carries. A promissory note missing the SSN match is a weaker result, because for that document type SSN is a critical expected signal. The LLM prompt included the document type classification and the expected field set for each type, enabling it to evaluate match quality relative to what was available — not penalizing for fields that would never appear on that kind of document.

**Confidence Threshold Calibration.** The auto-accept threshold wasn't set arbitrarily — it was calibrated at the intersection of two constraints: precision and human review capacity.

On the precision side, we ran the pipeline on a sample of documents that had already been matched by Tiers 1 and 2 — known correct matches — and measured what confidence scores the LLM assigned. This gave us the precision curve: at what threshold does auto-accept accuracy exceed 99%? In practice, this landed around 0.85.

On the capacity side, we had approximately 500 field reviewers allocated to the program, each capable of processing roughly 20 documents per day (substantially faster than the pre-AI baseline, thanks to the LLM-generated explanations). Over a 3-4 month window, that gave us a review capacity of approximately 700,000 documents. The Tier 3 population was roughly 6 million documents (~3% of 200M). That meant we could send at most 10-12% of Tier 3 to human review — the auto-accept threshold had to clear approximately 88-90% of the volume to keep the review queue within operational capacity.

Both constraints converged at roughly the same threshold. The precision target and the capacity constraint were satisfied simultaneously — which gave us confidence that the threshold was well-calibrated. We validated with ongoing spot-check sampling of auto-accepted matches, confirming the actual error rate stayed within the expected range throughout the program.

**Confidence-Based Routing.** High-confidence matches (above threshold) were auto-accepted, with the explanation logged directly to the audit trail. Everything else was routed to a single human review queue, prioritized by confidence score — hardest cases first, clearest cases last. Both paths wrote to the same immutable audit log in Snowflake.

### Human-in-the-Loop: Not a Fallback, a Feature

The human-in-the-loop (HITL) component was not a concession to imperfect automation. It was a deliberate architectural decision that served two purposes: better accuracy and governance.

**Better accuracy.** For documents in the review queue, analysts saw the document metadata, the top candidate account profiles, and the LLM-generated explanation articulating why the model chose a particular match. They could approve, reject, or reassign. The queue was prioritized by confidence score — borderline cases surfaced first, giving reviewers the ability to triage efficiently. This collaboration — vector retrieval for candidate identification, LLM for reasoning and explanation, human for final judgment — produced outcomes that were better than any single component operating alone. The LLM explanation reduced review time from approximately five minutes per document (raw metadata, no context) to roughly 45 seconds (pre-analyzed, with reasoning). The system made the human more effective, not redundant.

**Governance and auditability.** Every decision — auto-accepted or human-adjudicated — was logged to an immutable audit trail: document identifier, matched account, match method (deterministic / probabilistic / RAG-auto / RAG-reviewed), confidence score, LLM explanation text, reviewer ID (for human decisions), and timestamp. This wasn't built for a specific audit or examination. It was built to ensure that if the completeness or accuracy of the document transfer was ever questioned — whether six months or six years later — the full decision lineage was there for every document, ready to answer whatever question was asked. Crucially, the LLM-generated explanation meant that even auto-accepted matches had a human-readable rationale on file — not just a similarity score.

The HITL design reflected a principle we think applies broadly to AI-assisted decision-making in regulated environments: the goal isn't to remove humans from the loop — it's to make the loop faster, more informed, and fully traceable.

### Completeness Reporting and Audit Infrastructure

Matching documents to accounts was only half the problem. The other half was knowing *what should be there* and being able to prove, at any point in the future, that the transfer was complete and every decision was traceable.

<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 5: Completeness framework — mandatory documents are checked against each account, coverage is tracked in a matrix by document type, and a resolution waterfall progressively closes gaps through multiple strategies.
</span>

**Mandatory document coverage.** The mandatory document checklist varied by loan characteristics. Every borrower account needed a promissory note and a final disclosure. Accounts with cosigners additionally required a cosigner addendum and cosigner disclosure. Certain program types required custom graduation addendums. Tax forms were required for all accounts. Statements had to be present for recent periods. The completeness matrix — account by document type, updated weekly — became the operational heartbeat of the program. It surfaced coverage rates by document type, by vintage year, and by source system, making it immediately visible where the remaining gaps concentrated.

**Match-rate reporting by tier and method.** Every matched document carried a tag indicating *how* it was matched: deterministic (Tier 1), probabilistic (Tier 2), RAG-auto (Tier 3, auto-accepted), or RAG-reviewed (Tier 3, human-approved). This breakdown served two purposes. Operationally, it showed us where each tier was contributing — and where a tier was underperforming relative to expectation. For governance, it gave a clear picture of how much of the final matched population relied on AI-assisted methods versus traditional joins, and what proportion had been reviewed by a human.

**The immutable audit trail.** Every match decision — across all three tiers — was logged to an immutable table in Snowflake: document identifier, matched account, match method, confidence score, LLM explanation text (for Tier 3), reviewer ID (for human-adjudicated matches), and timestamp. The explanation field was particularly valuable: for Tier 3 matches, it provided a human-readable rationale that a non-technical examiner could evaluate, not just a numerical score.

**Gap resolution tracking.** Where mandatory documents were missing after all three matching tiers, the resolution waterfall tracked the progression: cross-source metadata reconciliation (the document exists in another system under a different categorization), document re-creation from templates (for documents like statements that could be regenerated from transactional data), the RAG pipeline (for documents that existed but couldn't be associated through structured methods), and manual investigation. Gap rates varied dramatically by vintage year — loans from the most recent decade had near-complete inventories, while early-2000s vintages had significantly higher gap rates. The waterfall reporting made it visible exactly how much each resolution layer contributed.

### Deploying for a One-Off Migration

A portfolio migration is inherently a time-boxed, one-off event. We are not matching documents to accounts on an ongoing basis — we are doing it once, thoroughly, and then retiring the tooling. This shaped every deployment decision. It also encompassed the final operational steps: centralizing matched documents into a staging repository, converting formats (proprietary archive to PDF, flat files to structured output), generating transfer rosters in the industry-standard format required by the receiving servicer, and executing the actual SFTP transfer aligned with each account conversion tranche.

<span style={{ fontSize: "0.85em", fontStyle: "italic" }}>
  Figure 6: Deployment reality — the one-off migration used managed services (Textract, Bedrock, Snowflake) with no GPUs or model hosting to manage, while a recurring workflow would require production-grade orchestration, monitoring, and drift detection.
</span>

**What we actually built.** Documents in S3 were processed through Amazon Textract for OCR. Extracted text was passed to AWS Bedrock for LLM-assisted field extraction and, later in the pipeline, for matching and explanation generation — using the same VPC-isolated, PrivateLink-routed deployment pattern we'd established for other AI workloads. Sensitive identifiers were tokenized through the enterprise data protection platform before reaching the cloud. Snowflake served triple duty: central metadata repository, vector index (native vector column with cosine similarity), and immutable audit trail. The total Tier 3 volume was roughly 6 million documents processed as batch API calls through Bedrock — no GPUs to provision, no model hosting, no inference infrastructure to manage. The human review application was purpose-built for this migration — a lightweight web interface with queues, approve/reject workflows, and direct writes to the audit log.

**The key principle: optimize for time-to-resolution, not operational robustness.** We needed the system to process the unmatched residual as accurately as possible within the migration timeline, not to run indefinitely. Every engineering decision was made through that lens — managed services, fewer moving parts, faster iteration when something needed adjustment.

**What a recurring workflow would look like.** If this were a recurring process — for example, an ongoing document classification system that continuously matches incoming documents to accounts — the deployment architecture would look quite different. You'd want a containerized embedding service with GPU backing for consistent throughput. A managed vector database with persistence, high availability, and automated backups. A pipeline orchestrator (Airflow, Step Functions) with retry logic, alerting, and DAG management. Model monitoring to detect embedding drift and confidence score degradation over time. And a continuous feedback loop where human review decisions inform model tuning. The infrastructure overhead is substantially higher, but it's justified when the system needs to run reliably for months or years. For a one-off migration, it would have been overengineering.

### What We Learned

**The metadata repository is the single highest-leverage investment.** Everything downstream — gap analysis, matching, transfer preparation, completeness reporting — depends on having a centralized, normalized view of what documents exist and where they live. Building this repository was the most time-consuming phase of the program, but it made everything else possible. If you're facing a similar migration, start here.

**Exhaust cheap methods before deploying expensive ones.** The three-tier cascade wasn't just a matching strategy — it was a cost and complexity management strategy. Deterministic matching resolved 85% of the population at essentially zero marginal cost. Probabilistic scoring resolved another 12%. By the time we reached the RAG pipeline, we were operating on roughly 3% of the original volume — a 30× reduction in GenAI scope. This principle generalizes: always structure your pipeline so that the most expensive techniques are applied to the smallest, most intractable population.

**Semantic search earns its place when the problem shifts from metadata to content.** For the portion of the residual where structured metadata fields were simply too sparse to work with, reading the actual document text was the only path forward. Embedding-based semantic search let us encode noisy OCR output, partial names, and date fragments into vectors and search across the account space in ways that no rule-based system could. This is the genuine use case — not a replacement for record linkage, but a complement for the cases that record linkage can't reach.

**RAG earns its place through explainability, not just accuracy.** Running every Tier 3 document through the full RAG pipeline was a deliberate choice. The LLM-generated explanation is what makes the match *auditable*. An examiner reviewing the audit trail years later can't evaluate "cosine similarity: 0.93." They can evaluate "Matched because borrower name matches OCR text, origination date is within 2 days, and SSN token aligns." The marginal cost of running every Tier 3 document through Bedrock was negligible compared to the governance value of having a human-readable rationale for every AI-assisted match decision.

**Text assembly is where semantic search succeeds or fails.** The embedding model doesn't understand database fields — it understands text. Constructing labeled, consistently structured text representations for both accounts and documents was the single most impactful engineering decision in Tier 3. The labels ("Borrower:", "Origination Date:", "Doc Type:") act as semantic anchors that enable the model to recognize cross-format matches between structured account profiles and unstructured OCR output. Get the templates wrong, and similarity search returns noise. Get them right, and a scanned promissory note from 2003 lands next to the account it belongs to.

**Human-in-the-loop is not a fallback — it's a feature.** The adjudication dashboard wasn't a concession to imperfect automation. Analysts reviewing AI-proposed matches with LLM-generated reasoning were faster and more accurate than analysts reviewing raw document metadata without context. In a regulated environment, the combination of AI-generated explanations and human approval created a governance model that neither pure automation nor pure manual review could match.

**Build the audit trail from day one.** We didn't know whether regulators would ask to see our document matching methodology six months or six years after the transfer. We built the immutable audit trail anyway — logging every decision with full lineage — because the cost of adding it later would have been orders of magnitude higher than building it in from the start. Proactive governance is always cheaper than reactive reconstruction.

**Vintage year is the strongest predictor of difficulty.** If your portfolio spans decades, expect that the oldest vintages will consume a disproportionate share of your gap resolution effort. Build your project plan around vintage-specific complexity, not average-case assumptions.

**Match your deployment to your timeline.** This was a one-off migration, and we deployed accordingly — managed services, existing infrastructure, purpose-built review tooling. Investing in production-grade ML infrastructure would have consumed time we didn't have for capability we didn't need. Know the difference between building for durability and building for a deadline.

### Conclusion

Matching a legacy lending portfolio's document inventory to borrower accounts — with its fragmented source systems, decades of accumulated metadata inconsistency, and near-zero tolerance for gaps — was the most complex data engineering challenge our team had faced. The structured account data migration, the part everyone worries about, was the straightforward half. The document matching, with its storage heterogeneity and completeness requirements, was where the real engineering happened.

The three-tier matching strategy — deterministic joins, probabilistic scoring, and the RAG pipeline — provided the resolution architecture and the cost management framework. The central metadata repository provided the foundation. The human-in-the-loop design ensured that AI-assisted decisions had governance, explainability, and a permanent audit trail. And the deployment approach — managed services, deliberately lightweight, matched to the one-off nature of the task — demonstrated that GenAI can deliver value in regulated environments without requiring a full production ML platform, as long as the scope is right and the humans stay in the loop.

If your organization is planning a portfolio transfer, a business line wind-down, or any large-scale document matching effort, the lesson from our experience is this: start with the documents, not the data. The data will follow well-worn patterns. The documents will surprise you — and the earlier you invest in understanding what you have, where it lives, and how to match it, the better your outcome will be.
